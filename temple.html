Kashi Vishwanath Temple

The golden-spired shrine of Lord Shiva at the spiritual centre of Varanasi — one of the twelve Jyotirlingas and the reason Kashi has drawn pilgrims for over three thousand years.

01 Overview

Kashi Vishwanath Temple sits on the western bank of the Ganga, its gold-plated spire visible above the rooftops of the old city. The current sanctum enshrines a Shiva lingam and is one of the twelve Jyotirlingas — sites where Shiva is believed to have manifested as a column of light. Since the 2019–2021 redevelopment, the temple now connects directly to Lalita Ghat via the Kashi Vishwanath Corridor, a wide sandstone concourse lined with galleries, a Vedic study centre and food courts.

02 Historical Story

Temples have stood on or near this site for well over a thousand years, repeatedly destroyed and rebuilt through cycles of invasion and reconstruction. The structure most visitors see today was commissioned in 1780 by Ahilyabai Holkar, the Maratha queen of Indore, after earlier temples were demolished in the 17th century. In 1839, Maharaja Ranjit Singh of Punjab donated roughly a tonne of gold to plate the temple's two main domes, giving Kashi Vishwanath its enduring nickname: the Golden Temple of Varanasi.

03 Why It Is Important

For Shaivite Hindus, Kashi Vishwanath is among the holiest sites on earth. Varanasi itself is considered Shiva's own city, and this temple is regarded as the point where liberation, or moksha, is most accessible to the devout. Millions of pilgrims visit annually, particularly during Shivratri and the month of Shravan, when the queue for darshan can stretch for hours through the corridor.

04 Timings

The temple opens before dawn for Mangala Aarti and stays active through the day with scheduled aartis, closing briefly between ceremonies. General darshan is available across most open hours, with the biggest crowds gathering for the Mangala Aarti and the late-evening Shringar and Shayan Aartis.

05 Entry Information

Entry is free for general darshan through the public queue; paid Sugam Darshan (fast-track) tickets are available online and at the counter for a shorter wait. Airport-style security screening applies at the corridor gates — phones are allowed, but leather items, bags and electronics beyond a phone should be left at the cloakroom outside.

06 Dress Code

Modest clothing is expected: shoulders and knees covered for all visitors. Traditional Indian attire is welcomed but not required. Footwear must be removed before entering the inner sanctum — free shoe-storage counters are available near the entry gates.

07 Photography Rules

Photography and mobile phones are not permitted inside the sanctum sanctorum itself. The outer corridor, galleries and the view of the Ganga from the temple complex are generally open for photography — look for posted signage at each checkpoint, as rules are enforced strictly and can change during major festivals.

08 Best Time to Visit

Early morning, right after the Mangala Aarti, offers the shortest queues and the softest light for photographs along the corridor. October to March brings cooler, more comfortable weather for the walk from the ghats. Avoid the Shravan month and Maha Shivratri unless you specifically want to experience the festival crowds.

09 Estimated Visit Duration

Budget 45 to 60 minutes for the general queue and darshan, and up to two hours during peak season or festival days. Add another 20 to 30 minutes if you plan to walk the full Kashi Vishwanath Corridor down to Lalita Ghat.
